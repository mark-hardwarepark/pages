Consolidated Competency Framework

13 parent competencies assessed at two levels — 10 through work products and deliverables, 3 through reflection, observation, and coaching — with progression from Aware through Applied to Integrated

Framework Architecture

Parent competencies are what gets tracked, assessed, and reported. These are the 13 things a fellow demonstrably develops over 12 months.

Sub-skills remain visible to fellows and staff as the specific practices and behaviors that compose each parent competency. They guide feedback conversations, self-reflection, and rubric development — but they don't get separate assessment lines.

Progression levels (Aware → Applied → Integrated) describe developmental depth. Fellows are expected to reach at least the Applied level in all competencies by the end of the fellowship, with Integrated representing exceptional depth.

Assessed Through Work Products and Deliverables

10 competencies
1
Empathy & Perspective-Driven Design
Understanding the people your work affects well enough to make better decisions for them — through observation, deliberate perspective-taking, and checking your own blind spots.
4 sub-skills ▶
Observation & need-finding
Watching people in context, suspending assumptions, and identifying unmet needs through direct engagement rather than desk research alone.
Stakeholder interviewing & need elicitation
Conducting structured and semi-structured interviews with users, clinicians, caregivers, and other stakeholders to surface unmet needs, pain points, and workarounds.
Stakeholder perspective-taking
Carrying empathy beyond initial observation into design decisions, concept evaluation, and testing strategy. Using tools like influence-interest mapping and value network diagrams.
Bias recognition in design & research
Checking blind spots and recognizing when assumptions, methods, or team composition systematically miss someone's experience.
2
Problem Framing & Reframing
Defining what problem is worth solving — questioning assumptions, reframing to expose better solution spaces, and applying first-principles reasoning to resist premature solutions.
2 sub-skills ▶
First-principles reasoning
Breaking problems down to fundamental components and building understanding from the ground up, rather than reasoning by analogy or convention.
Reframe generation
Deliberately producing alternative framings of the same problem to expand the solution space and avoid premature convergence.
3
AI Fluency
Directing, verifying, and integrating AI tools into professional work with skill and accountability.
4 sub-skills ▶
Prompt engineering & iterative direction
Structuring clear, context-rich prompts, providing constraints, and refining through iterative cycles to get increasingly useful outputs.
Output validation & calibrated skepticism
Evaluating AI outputs against known standards and domain knowledge. Developing an accurate sense of when AI tools are reliable versus confidently wrong.
AI-augmented workflow design
Identifying which parts of a workflow benefit from AI assistance and building repeatable processes that leverage AI while preserving human judgment.
Ethical ownership of AI outputs
Taking accountability for quality, accuracy, and representativeness of AI-generated work. A fellow's name on the work means they've vetted what the tool produced.
4
Evidence Provenance & Critical Interpretation
Understanding where evidence comes from, what it can and cannot support, and making sound inferences under uncertainty.
2 sub-skills ▶
Quantitative data provenance & interpretation
Evaluating measurement uncertainty, sample size, statistical validity, and test method assumptions. Understanding what numbers actually tell you and where they stop being reliable.
Research & source credibility assessment
Evaluating the reliability, methodology, and potential bias of published research, market reports, and other decision-driving sources. Calibrating confidence accordingly.
5
Technical & Stakeholder Communication
Communicating technical work effectively across audiences and formats — translating complexity into clarity without losing rigor.
4 sub-skills ▶
Translational communication
Explaining technical concepts to non-technical audiences and translating clinical, business, or regulatory concerns into language engineers can act on.
Structured argumentation & persuasion
Building clear, evidence-based cases for design decisions, project directions, or resource requests with followable logic and traceable evidence.
Sketch communication
Rapidly externalizing spatial, mechanical, or conceptual ideas through hand sketches during conversations and design reviews — a conversational tool, not a polished deliverable.
Data & visual communication
Presenting quantitative information and complex relationships through charts, diagrams, and designed visuals. Making data tell a clear story rather than just displaying numbers.
6
Systems & Integrative Reasoning
Seeing interactions across subsystems, translating across disciplines, and anticipating how changes propagate through complex systems.
3 sub-skills ▶
Systems thinking & feedback loop analysis
Identifying variables, relationships, and feedback loops that define system behavior. Using causal loop diagrams, recognizing delays, understanding why systems resist intervention.
Cross-disciplinary synthesis
Integrating knowledge and methods from different fields into coherent solutions. Recognizing when a problem requires expertise beyond your discipline and bringing perspectives together productively.
2nd & 3rd-order consequence modeling (awareness level)
Anticipating how a design decision, policy change, or intervention ripples beyond its immediate effect into adjacent systems.
7
Agile & Iterative Project Execution
Breaking work into testable increments, incorporating feedback loops, and treating each iteration as an opportunity to learn and adjust.
3 sub-skills ▶
Sprint planning & task decomposition
Breaking work into time-bounded, prioritized sprints with clear deliverables, estimating effort, and delivering on commitments within sprint cycles.
Clarifying ambiguity through iterative testing & learning loops
Moving forward productively when requirements are incomplete. Designing each iteration to answer a specific question, capturing learnings, and adjusting based on evidence.
Retrospective practice
Structured reflection on what worked, what didn't, and what to change — applied to the process itself, not just the product.
8
Regulatory & Compliance Literacy
Understanding how innovation interacts with governance frameworks — designing with regulatory and quality constraints from the start.
3 sub-skills ▶
Regulatory pathway analysis
Understanding clearance and approval mechanisms, identifying the most appropriate pathway for a given product, and articulating the rationale. Knowing the difference between 510(k), De Novo, and PMA.
Design controls & documentation practice
Working within a design control framework from the start, understanding that how you develop is as important as what you develop. Documentation that serves both the team and future regulatory submissions.
Risk management fundamentals
Applying structured risk analysis to design decisions, understanding ISO 14971 basics, and integrating risk thinking into everyday engineering judgment.
9
Venture Economics
Tracing the financial implications of technical decisions and the technical implications of financial constraints. Every technical decision is simultaneously an economic one.
4 sub-skills ▶
Market & competitive intelligence
Understanding market size, segmentation, competitive positioning, pricing dynamics, and buying processes. Evaluating competitive products on commercial positioning, not just technical merit.
Problem economics & viability assessment
Evaluating whether the economics of a problem justify pursuing a solution. Sizing cost burden, identifying who pays, understanding reimbursement pathways.
Product economic modeling
Building rough but honest estimates of development cost, unit economics, and capital requirements. Thinking through concept-to-clearance costs, unit costs, minimum viable pricing, and margin structure.
Financial dimensions of design decisions
Evaluating technical tradeoffs with cost alongside performance, manufacturability, and regulatory considerations. Includes target costing, DFM, burn rate awareness, and build-vs-license-vs-partner reasoning.
10
Patent & IP Intelligence
Understanding and navigating the intellectual property landscape to inform whether and how to pursue a project, using AI tools to conduct research and critical judgment to interpret results.
4 sub-skills ▶
Patent system literacy
Understanding provisional vs. non-provisional applications, utility vs. design patents, patentability, filing timelines, maintenance requirements, and term lengths.
Patent landscape research
Searching patent databases, identifying relevant patents, pulling key information (claims, filing dates, assignees, patent families), and summarizing the landscape for go/no-go decisions.
Freedom-to-operate assessment
Understanding what freedom to operate means, evaluating whether a concept falls within existing patent claims, and flagging potential conflicts for expert review.
IP-aware documentation practices
Maintaining engineering notebooks and design records that support potential patent filings. Understanding inventorship, dates of conception and reduction to practice.

Assessed Through Reflection, Observation, and Coaching

3 competencies
11
Collaborative Professional Practice
Working generatively with others — building on ideas, giving actionable feedback, and navigating disagreement as a productive force rather than a threat.
3 sub-skills ▶
Co-creation & generative collaboration
Contributing to shared work in ways that build on others' ideas rather than just defending your own. Thinking together rather than dividing and conquering.
Feedback as professional practice
Giving specific, actionable feedback that helps others improve, and receiving feedback without defensiveness. Treating feedback as normal professional work.
Navigating conflict & disagreement productively
Engaging with disagreement as a source of better decisions. Distinguishing productive tension about ideas from unproductive friction between people.
12
Professional Growth & Adaptability
Learning fast, moving forward under uncertainty, and taking ownership of your own development trajectory.
3 sub-skills ▶
Adaptive learning & framework transfer
Learning new concepts, tools, and methods quickly and applying frameworks from one domain to unfamiliar problems. Learning what you need when you need it.
Operating under ambiguity
Making progress when the path isn't clear, requirements aren't fully defined, or information is incomplete. Tolerating uncertainty without freezing or rushing to premature closure.
Self-directed professional development
Identifying your own skill gaps, seeking out learning opportunities, and taking initiative for growth rather than waiting for assigned development activities.
13
Ethical Reasoning & Responsible Innovation
Recognizing ethical dimensions in real work and reasoning through genuine moral complexity — practical judgment that shapes design and development decisions.
4 sub-skills ▶
Responsible innovation & foresight
Recognizing when decisions carry ethical weight and proactively considering downstream consequences before problems emerge. Building the habit of asking "should we?" alongside "can we?"
Understanding AI limitations by domain
Exercising judgment about when AI tools are and aren't appropriate based on stakes, domain, and tool reliability. Recognizing that effectiveness for brainstorming doesn't imply reliability for regulatory research.
Ethical reasoning under competing values
Working through genuine moral tradeoffs where legitimate values conflict — access vs. affordability, speed vs. thoroughness, innovation vs. precaution.
2nd & 3rd-order consequence modeling
Anticipating how innovations ripple beyond intended use into broader systems. Moving beyond "does this work?" to "what happens when this works at scale?" At the awareness level: developing the habit of asking "and then what happens?"

Four Thematic Groups

The 13 competencies cluster naturally into four groups that describe the arc of what fellows learn: understanding problems deeply, making informed decisions, delivering real work in regulated environments, and growing as professionals. This grouping cuts across the assessment-method categories — each group may include competencies assessed through work products as well as through reflection and coaching.

Progression Levels: Aware → Applied → Integrated

Each competency is assessed against a three-level progression rubric. Aware means the fellow recognizes the concept and can apply it with guidance. Applied means they use it independently in their project work. Integrated means the competency is embedded in how they approach every problem — it's second nature. Fellows are expected to reach at least Applied by the end of 12 months.

1
Empathy & Perspective-Driven Design
▶
Aware
Conducts basic observation sessions and can identify obvious unmet needs when guided. Recognizes that different stakeholders have different perspectives. Understands the concept of bias in design but needs prompting to check for it.
Applied
Plans and conducts observation sessions and stakeholder interviews independently. Uses perspective-taking tools (influence-interest maps, value network diagrams) in project work. Proactively identifies potential blind spots in research methods and team composition.
Integrated
Empathy is embedded in every phase of project work — from problem identification through concept evaluation and testing. Teaches observation and interviewing techniques to others. Routinely surfaces bias concerns the team hasn't considered and adjusts methods to address them.
2
Problem Framing & Reframing
▶
Aware
Recognizes that problem framing affects the solution space. Can identify assumptions in a given problem statement when prompted. Understands first-principles reasoning conceptually but defaults to reasoning by analogy.
Applied
Independently produces multiple framings of the same problem. Uses first-principles reasoning to question inherited assumptions and identify true constraints vs. conventions. Generates reframes that meaningfully change the solution space.
Integrated
Instinctively questions problem framings before committing to a solution direction. Helps the team resist premature convergence. Produces reframes that have demonstrably opened better solution paths in project work.
3
AI Fluency
▶
Aware
Uses AI tools for basic tasks with simple prompts. Understands that AI outputs need verification but inconsistently checks them. Recognizes the concept of ethical responsibility for AI-generated content.
Applied
Writes structured, context-rich prompts and iterates effectively. Has developed reliable validation habits and can identify when AI outputs are unreliable. Designs repeatable workflows that integrate AI where it adds value. Takes full ownership of AI-generated work products.
Integrated
AI is a natural extension of their professional practice. Designs novel AI-augmented workflows for the team. Teaches effective AI use to others. Has a calibrated, domain-specific sense of when to trust, verify, or bypass AI tools.
4
Evidence Provenance & Critical Interpretation
▶
Aware
Conducts basic literature searches and distinguishes between primary and secondary sources. Understands that evidence quality varies. Can identify obvious weaknesses in a source when prompted.
Applied
Evaluates source quality independently. Produces analyses that explicitly address evidence gaps and uncertainty. Understands measurement limitations and statistical validity. Maintains organized reference management.
Integrated
Evidence evaluation is embedded in their approach to every project. Proactively identifies when evidence is insufficient to support a decision, seeks out disconfirming evidence, and helps the team calibrate confidence appropriately.
5
Technical & Stakeholder Communication
▶
Aware
Communicates technical information clearly within their own discipline. Uses basic sketches and visuals. Can present work to a familiar audience. Understands that different audiences need different communication approaches.
Applied
Translates effectively between technical and non-technical audiences. Builds structured arguments for design decisions with traceable evidence. Uses sketch communication as a real-time conversational tool. Produces data visualizations that tell clear stories.
Integrated
Moves fluently between disciplinary vocabularies without losing rigor. Communication quality elevates team decision-making. Adapts format, depth, and framing instinctively to audience and context. Others seek out their help communicating complex ideas.
6
Systems & Integrative Reasoning
▶
Aware
Recognizes that systems have interconnected components. Can identify basic feedback loops when guided. Understands that solutions in one area can create problems elsewhere. Seeks input from other disciplines.
Applied
Uses systems diagrams (causal loop, stock-and-flow) to make system dynamics visible. Independently identifies reinforcing and balancing loops. Integrates perspectives from multiple disciplines into coherent project recommendations. Anticipates first-order consequences of design decisions.
Integrated
Systems thinking is a natural lens for all project work. Surfaces non-obvious interactions that the team missed. Anticipates second and third-order consequences. Facilitates cross-disciplinary conversations that produce genuinely integrated solutions.
7
Agile & Iterative Project Execution
▶
Aware
Understands sprint-based workflow. Can break a large task into smaller pieces with guidance. Participates in retrospectives. Understands the concept of iterative testing but may default to building before testing.
Applied
Plans and executes sprints independently. Designs iterations to answer specific questions. Accurately estimates effort for familiar task types. Leads retrospectives and implements process changes. Makes work visible through consistent documentation.
Integrated
Agile practice is embedded in how they approach all work. Helps the team maintain sprint discipline when pressure mounts. Uses retrospective insights to improve team-level processes. Balances speed with rigor instinctively.
8
Regulatory & Compliance Literacy
▶
Aware
Knows that medical devices require regulatory clearance/approval. Can distinguish between major pathway types (510(k), De Novo, PMA). Understands that documentation matters for compliance. Recognizes risk management as a regulatory requirement.
Applied
Analyzes regulatory pathways for specific products and articulates pathway rationale. Produces design documentation that would be useful in a regulatory submission. Applies ISO 14971 risk management principles to design decisions. Works within a design control framework naturally.
Integrated
Regulatory and quality thinking is embedded in every design decision from the start. Helps the team see regulatory requirements as design constraints that shape better solutions, not bureaucratic obstacles. Risk management is a natural part of engineering judgment.
9
Venture Economics
▶
Aware
Understands that technical decisions have cost implications. Can identify basic market information (size, competitors, pricing) with guidance. Knows that medical device reimbursement affects viability. Understands the concept of unit economics.
Applied
Produces competitive analyses that inform positioning decisions. Evaluates problem economics independently — sizing cost burden, identifying payers, assessing viability. Builds rough product economic models. Evaluates design tradeoffs with cost as an explicit dimension. Understands target costing and DFM implications.
Integrated
Economic reasoning is embedded in every technical decision. Naturally asks "what does this cost?" and "who pays?" alongside "does this work?" Produces financial analyses that influence project direction. Has an intuitive sense of burn rate and capital implications of scope changes.
10
Patent & IP Intelligence
▶
Aware
Understands basic patent types and that IP matters for ventures. Can search patent databases with guidance. Knows that engineering notebooks support patent filings. Recognizes the concept of freedom to operate.
Applied
Conducts independent patent landscape research and summarizes findings for go/no-go decisions. Evaluates whether concepts may fall within existing patent claims. Maintains IP-aware documentation practices. Understands filing timelines and strategic implications.
Integrated
IP awareness is embedded in their innovation practice. Proactively identifies potential IP issues early in development. Produces patent landscape analyses that materially inform project strategy. Flags freedom-to-operate concerns before the team has committed resources.
11
Collaborative Professional Practice
▶
Aware
Works cooperatively on team tasks. Gives basic feedback when asked. Avoids conflict rather than engaging with it productively. Contributes ideas but may struggle to build on others' contributions.
Applied
Actively builds on others' ideas in collaborative work sessions. Gives specific, actionable feedback unprompted. Engages with disagreement constructively — surfaces concerns without escalating. Receives critical feedback without defensiveness.
Integrated
Consistently elevates team output through collaborative practice. Others seek out their feedback. Navigates conflict in ways that produce better outcomes. Models feedback culture for the cohort. Creates psychological safety in collaborative settings.
12
Professional Growth & Adaptability
▶
Aware
Learns new tools and methods when directed. Can identify skill gaps when prompted. Prefers clear instructions and defined scope. May struggle when requirements are ambiguous or changing.
Applied
Learns new domains and tools quickly without hand-holding. Makes productive progress under ambiguity — asks clarifying questions, runs small experiments, and moves forward without waiting for complete information. Identifies own skill gaps and seeks learning opportunities proactively.
Integrated
Thrives in ambiguous environments. Transfers frameworks from one domain to another naturally. Drives their own professional development with a clear sense of trajectory. Models adaptive learning for the cohort. Comfortable saying "I don't know this yet" and efficiently closing the gap.
13
Ethical Reasoning & Responsible Innovation
▶
Aware
Recognizes that design decisions can have ethical implications. Understands that AI tools have domain-specific limitations. Can identify obvious ethical concerns when prompted. Beginning to ask "should we?" alongside "can we?"
Applied
Independently identifies ethical dimensions in project work. Exercises informed judgment about AI tool appropriateness by domain. Engages with genuine moral tradeoffs rather than treating ethics as a checklist. Anticipates first-order downstream consequences of innovations.
Integrated
Ethical reasoning is embedded in their approach to every project. Surfaces ethical considerations the team hasn't identified. Models responsible AI use for the cohort. Traces second and third-order consequences of design decisions as a natural part of evaluation. Helps the team navigate genuine moral complexity.
