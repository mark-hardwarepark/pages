What You'll Learn

The Biodesign Fellowship develops 13 competencies that make you a more effective engineer, designer, and innovator — skills that employers need and that become more valuable as the world changes around you.

How we chose these competencies

We didn't pick these from a textbook. We built this framework by looking at three things at once — and keeping only the competencies where all three pointed in the same direction.

▲ Future-facing skills

We studied which engineering and design skills are becoming more valuable as AI tools grow more capable — and focused on the ones that appreciate rather than depreciate.

✚ What employers need

We analyzed workforce data, employer surveys, and hiring trends to identify the skills companies struggle to find — especially in engineering, design, and regulated industries.

◎ Biodesign process

Every competency maps to the Stanford Biodesign innovation process — the methodology you'll use to take real medical technologies from concept to market.

Understanding the Problem

2 competencies
1

Empathy & Perspective-Driven Design

Understanding the people your work affects well enough to make better decisions for them — through observation, deliberate perspective-taking, and checking your own blind spots.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Observation & need-finding
Watching people in context, suspending assumptions, and identifying unmet needs through direct engagement rather than desk research alone.
Stakeholder interviewing & need elicitation
Conducting interviews with users, clinicians, caregivers, and other stakeholders to surface pain points and workarounds they may not even realize they have.
Stakeholder perspective-taking
Carrying empathy into design decisions, concept evaluation, and testing — understanding that different people have different needs, incentives, and power.
Bias recognition in design & research
Checking your own blind spots — asking who isn't represented, whose perspective hasn't been sought, and whether design choices inadvertently exclude someone.
2

Problem Framing & Reframing

Defining what problem is worth solving — questioning assumptions, reframing to expose better solution spaces, and applying first-principles reasoning to resist premature solutions.

▶
What this looks like in practice
First-principles reasoning
Breaking problems down to their fundamental components and building understanding from the ground up, rather than reasoning by analogy or convention.
Reframe generation
Deliberately producing alternative framings of the same problem to expand the solution space and avoid locking in too early.

Making Smart Decisions

5 competencies
3

AI Fluency

Directing, verifying, and integrating AI tools into professional work with skill and accountability.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Prompt engineering & iterative direction
Structuring clear, context-rich prompts and refining them through iterative cycles to get increasingly useful outputs from AI tools.
Output validation & calibrated skepticism
Developing an accurate sense of when AI tools are reliable and when they're confidently wrong — rather than defaulting to blind trust or blanket distrust.
AI-augmented workflow design
Identifying which parts of a workflow benefit from AI assistance and building repeatable processes that leverage AI while preserving human judgment.
Ethical ownership of AI outputs
Your name on the work means you've vetted what the tool produced — taking accountability for quality, accuracy, and representativeness.
4

Evidence Provenance & Critical Interpretation

Understanding where evidence comes from, what it can and cannot support, and making sound inferences under uncertainty.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Quantitative data provenance & interpretation
Understanding what the numbers actually tell you and where they stop being reliable — measurement uncertainty, sample size, statistical validity.
Research & source credibility assessment
Knowing the difference between a peer-reviewed multicenter trial and a vendor white paper, and calibrating your confidence accordingly.
6

Systems & Integrative Reasoning

Seeing interactions across subsystems, translating across disciplines, and anticipating how changes propagate through complex systems.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Systems thinking & feedback loop analysis
Identifying the variables, relationships, and feedback loops that define how a system behaves — and understanding why systems often resist intervention.
Cross-disciplinary synthesis
Integrating knowledge from different fields into coherent solutions — and knowing when a problem requires expertise beyond your own discipline.
2nd & 3rd-order consequence modeling
Anticipating how a design decision ripples beyond its immediate effect into adjacent systems — asking "and then what happens?"
9

Venture Economics

Tracing the financial implications of technical decisions and the technical implications of financial constraints — because every technical decision is simultaneously an economic one.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Market & competitive intelligence
Understanding market size, competitive positioning, and buying processes — evaluating competitors on commercial positioning, not just technical merit.
Problem economics & viability assessment
Evaluating whether the economics of a problem justify pursuing a solution — sizing cost burden, identifying who pays, and understanding reimbursement pathways.
Product economic modeling
Building rough but honest estimates of development cost, unit economics, and capital requirements — what it actually costs to get from concept to cleared product.
Financial dimensions of design decisions
Evaluating technical tradeoffs with cost alongside performance — including target costing, design for manufacturing, and build-vs-license-vs-partner reasoning.
10

Patent & IP Intelligence

Understanding and navigating the intellectual property landscape — using AI tools to conduct research and applying critical judgment to interpret results.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Patent system literacy
Understanding provisional vs. non-provisional applications, utility vs. design patents, filing timelines, and how protection is obtained and sustained.
Patent landscape research
Searching patent databases, identifying relevant patents, and summarizing the landscape in a way that informs a go/no-go decision.
Freedom-to-operate assessment
Evaluating whether a proposed concept falls within existing patent claims and flagging potential conflicts for deeper expert review.
IP-aware documentation practices
Maintaining engineering notebooks and design records that support potential patent filings — understanding inventorship and documenting conception.

Delivering Real Work

3 competencies
5

Technical & Stakeholder Communication

Communicating technical work effectively across audiences and formats — translating complexity into clarity without losing rigor.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Translational communication
Moving fluently between disciplinary vocabularies — explaining technical concepts to non-technical audiences without dumbing down or overcomplicating.
Structured argumentation & persuasion
Building clear, evidence-based cases for design decisions with followable logic and traceable evidence — in memos, slide decks, or live presentations.
Sketch communication
Rapidly externalizing ideas through hand sketches during conversations and design reviews — a real-time conversational tool, not a polished deliverable.
Data & visual communication
Making data tell a clear story rather than just displaying numbers — charts, diagrams, and designed visuals that communicate relationships.
7

Agile & Iterative Project Execution

Breaking work into testable increments, incorporating feedback loops, and treating each iteration as an opportunity to learn and adjust.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Sprint planning & task decomposition
Breaking work into time-bounded, prioritized sprints with clear deliverables — making the work visible and manageable.
Clarifying ambiguity through iterative testing
Moving forward productively when requirements are incomplete — designing each iteration to answer a specific question, then adjusting based on evidence.
Retrospective practice
Structured reflection on what worked, what didn't, and what to change — improving how you work, not just what you produce.
8

Regulatory & Compliance Literacy

Understanding how innovation interacts with governance frameworks — designing with regulatory and quality constraints from the start rather than treating them as obstacles.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Regulatory pathway analysis
Understanding clearance and approval mechanisms — knowing the difference between 510(k), De Novo, and PMA, and how pathway selection shapes development strategy.
Design controls & documentation practice
Working within a design control framework from the start — understanding that how you develop is as important as what you develop.
Risk management fundamentals
Applying structured risk analysis to design decisions and integrating risk thinking into everyday engineering judgment.

Growing as a Professional

3 competencies
11

Collaborative Professional Practice

Working generatively with others — building on ideas, giving actionable feedback, and navigating disagreement as a productive force rather than a threat.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Co-creation & generative collaboration
Contributing to shared work in ways that build on others' ideas — thinking together rather than just dividing and conquering.
Feedback as professional practice
Giving specific, actionable feedback that helps others improve, and receiving feedback without defensiveness — treating it as a normal part of work.
Navigating conflict & disagreement productively
Engaging with disagreement as a source of better decisions — distinguishing productive tension about ideas from unproductive friction between people.
12

Professional Growth & Adaptability

Learning fast, moving forward under uncertainty, and taking ownership of your own development trajectory.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Adaptive learning & framework transfer
Learning what you need when you need it — applying frameworks from one domain to unfamiliar problems rather than relying only on what you already know.
Operating under ambiguity
Making progress when the path isn't clear, the requirements aren't defined, or the information is incomplete — without freezing or rushing to premature closure.
Self-directed professional development
Identifying your own skill gaps, seeking out learning opportunities, and taking initiative for your growth rather than waiting for someone to assign it.
13

Ethical Reasoning & Responsible Innovation

Recognizing ethical dimensions in real work and reasoning through genuine moral complexity — practical judgment that shapes design and development decisions.

▶
What this looks like in practice
Responsible innovation & foresight
Proactively considering downstream consequences before problems emerge — building the habit of asking "should we?" alongside "can we?"
Understanding AI limitations by domain
Recognizing that a tool effective for brainstorming may be unreliable for regulatory research — making informed choices about where to rely on AI versus where human expertise is non-negotiable.
Ethical reasoning under competing values
Working through genuine moral tradeoffs where legitimate values conflict — access vs. affordability, speed vs. thoroughness, innovation vs. precaution.
2nd & 3rd-order consequence modeling
Moving beyond "does this work?" to "what happens when this works at scale, and who is affected in ways we didn't intend?"
