Multi-Lens Competency Validation

Three independent lenses validating the 13-competency framework for the Hardware Park Biodesign Fellowship. Each lens asks a different question about why these competencies matter.

◆ Lens 1 — AI Era / Forward-Looking Skills ◆ Lens 2 — STEM Employer Needs ◆ Lens 3 — Biodesign Process Mapping

Why Three Lenses?

A competency framework is only as strong as the reasoning behind it. Rather than relying on a single justification for each competency, we validated the framework through three independent lenses — each asking a different question about why these 13 competencies matter.

Lens 1 — AI Era / Forward-Looking Skills asks which human skills become more valuable as AI tools grow more capable. Lens 2 — STEM Employer Needs asks whether each competency addresses a documented hiring gap or workforce shortage. Lens 3 — Biodesign Process Mapping asks where in the Stanford Biodesign process each competency gets developed and exercised. When all three lenses converge on the same competency, the case for its inclusion is strongest.

The 13 competencies are organized into four thematic groups that describe how they work together in practice:

What This Mapping Reveals

All 13 competencies are validated by at least two of the three lenses, and 10 of 13 are validated by all three. The framework is not redundant with any single perspective — it synthesizes forward-looking skill demands, documented employer hiring gaps, and process-grounded development opportunities into a unified structure.

The strongest convergence appears in Problem Framing, Evidence Provenance, Technical Communication, and Agile Execution — competencies where AI-era demands, biodesign process stages, and employer skill gaps all point in the same direction. This convergence gives the fellowship's program design strong external validation.

Lens 1 — AI Era / Forward-Looking Skills

Question this lens asks: Why does each competency matter in a world where AI tools are increasingly capable? Which skills become more valuable as AI automates routine technical work?

The World Economic Forum projects that 39% of core skills will change by 2030. AI and big data are the fastest-growing skill category. But the skills that complement AI — analytical thinking, creative problem-solving, empathy, systems thinking — are rising just as fast. This lens validates that the fellowship develops skills that appreciate rather than depreciate in an AI-augmented world.

1

Empathy & Perspective-Driven Design

Work Products

AI cannot enter a clinical environment, observe a patient struggling with a device, or sense the frustration a nurse won't articulate. Embodied empathy — physically present, emotionally attuned observation — is uniquely human and the starting point of meaningful innovation.

▲WEF: Empathy and active listening are top-10 core skills for 2025. As AI handles analytical tasks, the human capacity for understanding unspoken needs becomes the critical differentiator.
2

Problem Framing & Reframing

Work Products

AI can generate thousands of solutions, but it cannot determine which problem is worth solving. Problem framing — defining constraints, questioning assumptions, reframing from first principles — is the irreplaceable human contribution that determines whether AI outputs are valuable or noise.

▲WEF: Analytical thinking is the #1 core skill, rated essential by 70% of companies. This skill becomes more valuable as AI handles execution — the bottleneck shifts to defining the right question.
3

AI Fluency

Work Products

The ability to direct AI tools effectively — prompt engineering, output validation, and workflow integration — is the foundational skill of the AI era. Fellows who can orchestrate AI as a design and research collaborator multiply their output without sacrificing quality.

▲AI fluency demand grew 7x from 2023 to 2025. Gartner: 80% of engineers must upskill by 2027. McKinsey: 75% of knowledge workers already use AI tools informally.
4

Evidence Provenance & Critical Interpretation

Work Products

AI generates plausible-sounding content that may be fabricated, outdated, or biased. The ability to trace data to its source, assess credibility, detect hallucinations, and maintain evidentiary standards is a critical human check on AI outputs.

▲PwC: Industries most exposed to AI see 3x higher productivity growth — but only when outputs are validated. Data quality and provenance are the governance layer AI cannot self-provide.
5

Technical & Stakeholder Communication

Work Products

AI can draft text, but it cannot read a boardroom, calibrate a message for a skeptical FDA reviewer, or build trust with a clinician through sustained relationship. Translational communication — converting between technical, business, and clinical vocabularies — remains deeply human.

▲NACE 2025: Communication is rated 4.55/5 in importance by employers, but graduates score only 3.62. The gap is widening as digital-native graduates have less in-person communication practice.
6

Systems & Integrative Reasoning

Work Products

AI excels at local optimization but struggles with emergent system behavior, feedback loops, and unintended consequences. Humans who can reason across domains — clinical, technical, regulatory, financial — and anticipate second-order effects provide the integrative intelligence that AI cannot.

▲WEF 2025: Systems thinking is among the top-10 skills rising in importance. AI integration and information processing technologies are driving demand for critical systems-level problem solving.
7

Agile & Iterative Project Execution

Work Products

AI dramatically shortens cycle times for prototyping, testing, and iteration. This makes agile methods more powerful — you can run more cycles faster — but only if you know how to structure sprints, manage backlogs, and make scope decisions. AI amplifies agile execution; it doesn't replace the human discipline behind it.

▲IMF: Countries with higher adoption of new skills see 1.3% employment growth per percentage point increase. Agile methods are the organizational framework that channels AI-enabled speed into structured delivery.
8

Regulatory & Compliance Literacy

Work Products

As AI governance frameworks emerge globally, the regulatory mindset — understanding compliance architecture, documentation requirements, and design controls — transfers directly. Fellows who learn regulatory thinking through FDA/ISO contexts can apply the same mental models to AI governance, data privacy, and emerging technology regulation.

▲WEF: Quality control and regulatory skills are among the most prominent skills differentiating growing from declining jobs. AI governance roles are emerging rapidly across all industries.
9

Venture Economics

Work Products

AI can build financial models and forecast markets, but it cannot evaluate whether a venture is worth pursuing, assess founder-market fit, or make the judgment calls that determine go/no-go decisions. Financial reasoning in context — cost awareness in design, unit economics in product planning — is a human integration skill.

▲PwC: AI-exposed industries see 4x higher productivity growth. Understanding how to evaluate and capture that value — venture economics — ensures AI investments translate into sustainable businesses.
10

Patent & IP Intelligence

Work Products

AI tools can search patent databases and identify prior art, but they cannot assess strategic IP positioning, evaluate freedom-to-operate risk, or determine when to file versus when to maintain trade secrets. Patent intelligence requires judgment about competitive landscape, commercial strategy, and invention disclosure timing that AI cannot provide.

▲AI is accelerating prior art search and patent drafting, increasing the volume of IP activity. Professionals who understand IP strategy — not just IP search — become more valuable as the speed of innovation increases.
11

Collaborative Professional Practice

Reflection & Coaching

AI enables new collaboration modes — shared workspaces, asynchronous review, automated synthesis — but the human core of collaboration remains: building trust, navigating disagreement productively, giving feedback that lands, and co-creating solutions that no individual would reach alone.

▲WEF 2025: Leadership and social influence are the #3 core skill. As AI automates individual tasks, the ability to coordinate and lead human teams becomes the primary differentiator.
12

Professional Growth & Adaptability

Reflection & Coaching

The half-life of technical skills is shrinking as AI tools evolve rapidly. The meta-skill — learning how to learn, transferring frameworks across domains, operating productively under ambiguity — is what keeps professionals relevant as specific tools change. This is the skill that makes all other skills renewable.

▲WEF: Resilience, flexibility and agility are the #2 core skill. Curiosity and lifelong learning are #8 and rising. 59 out of every 100 workers will need training by 2030. Adaptability is the ultimate future-proof skill.
13

Ethical Reasoning & Responsible Innovation

Reflection & Coaching

AI raises novel ethical questions — algorithmic bias, data privacy, equitable access to medical technologies — that require human moral judgment. The ability to identify ethical tensions in design decisions, reason through competing stakeholder interests, and advocate for responsible innovation practices cannot be automated or delegated to technical systems.

▲WEF 2025: Responsible AI and ethical technology governance are among the fastest-emerging employer requirements. Companies increasingly seek professionals who can navigate ethical complexity as a core engineering skill, not just a compliance checkbox.

Key Patterns

Evidence Provenance (#4) and Technical Communication (#5) are the most broadly developed — showing secondary or primary intensity across all six stages. These are safe bets for early mastery expectations.

Empathy & Perspective-Driven Design (#1) is the most stage-concentrated. It peaks in Needs Finding and drops off sharply. If portfolio company work skips needs-finding, this competency requires deliberate curriculum design.

Regulatory Literacy (#8) and Patent & IP Intelligence (#10) concentrate in the back half — Concept Screening through Implementation. Fellows entering project work at early biodesign stages may need supplementary exposure.

Venture Economics (#9) peaks at Strategy Development — the stage where business model work is most intensive. Seminar programming should align financial literacy content with strategy-oriented project work.

Ethical Reasoning (#13) peaks at Concept Screening — the stage where go/no-go decisions carry the highest ethical stakes. But ethical awareness is needed from the earliest needs-finding observations through implementation.

Lens 2 — STEM Employer Needs

Question this lens asks: Does each competency address a documented employer hiring gap or workforce need? Are fellows developing the skills that employers actually struggle to find?

This lens draws on NACE Job Outlook surveys, the WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025, Bureau of Labor Statistics projections, and industry-specific workforce analyses. The consistent finding: employers face both a technical skills shortage and a critical gap in professional competencies like communication, problem-solving, and teamwork.

1

Empathy & Perspective-Driven Design

Work Products

Employers consistently report that engineering graduates struggle to understand end-user needs. The ability to move beyond technical requirements to genuine user empathy — understanding why problems matter, not just what they are — separates adequate engineers from exceptional ones.

✚WEF: Empathy and active listening are a top-10 core skill. Design and user experience skills are rising. Younger graduates show less in-person empathy practice due to digital communication patterns.
2

Problem Framing & Reframing

Work Products

Problem-solving is the #1 skill employers seek. But employers report a significant gap: graduates default to executing on given problems rather than questioning assumptions, reframing constraints, or identifying the right problem to solve in the first place.

✚NACE Job Outlook 2025: Problem-solving is the #1 sought skill. WEF: Analytical thinking rated essential by 70% of companies. Only 51.5% of undergraduates identify problem-solving as a critical skill.
3

AI Fluency

Work Products

Demand for AI fluency has exploded across all industries, not just tech. Employers need workers who can use AI tools productively, not just understand AI conceptually. The gap between AI-ready and AI-unready workers is creating a two-tier labor market.

✚McKinsey: Workers in AI-fluency roles grew from ~1M (2023) to ~7M (2025). Gartner: 80% of engineers must upskill by 2027. PwC: AI-exposed industries see 4x productivity growth.
4

Evidence Provenance & Critical Interpretation

Work Products

Data literacy is foundational to evidence-based engineering, quality management, and regulatory compliance. Employers need graduates who can collect, interpret, and draw defensible conclusions from data — not just run statistical tests, but understand what the data actually means in context.

✚WEF: AI and big data are the #1 fastest-growing skills. BLS: Data scientist employment projected to grow 34% from 2024–2034. Data quality and governance are emerging as board-level priorities.
5

Technical & Stakeholder Communication

Work Products

The communication skills gap is the most well-documented employer complaint about engineering graduates. Employers rate communication as the most essential career readiness competency, but graduates consistently underperform against this benchmark across written, verbal, and visual communication.

✚NACE: Communication rated 4.55/5 importance, graduates score 3.62. 65% of employers say graduates lack adequate communication skills. 80% of employers rate communication as critical for new hires.
6

Systems & Integrative Reasoning

Work Products

Employers need engineers who see beyond their discipline. The ability to reason across technical, business, regulatory, and user domains — to understand how a design decision in one area cascades through the whole system — is what separates senior engineers from junior ones, and it's in critically short supply among new graduates.

✚WEF: Systems thinking is a top-10 rising skill. Cross-training across domains (e.g., software engineers learning hardware) is an emerging employer strategy for building adaptable teams.
7

Agile & Iterative Project Execution

Work Products

Engineering employers face a 3:1 ratio of open positions to qualified candidates. Project management, scope definition, and iterative delivery skills are particularly scarce among recent graduates who have experience with academic projects but not production-environment engineering.

✚Addison Group: 3 engineering jobs per 1 qualified candidate. BLS: Engineering occupations growing steadily. 77% of employers have difficulty finding qualified engineering candidates (NACE 2023).
8

Regulatory & Compliance Literacy

Work Products

Quality and regulatory skills are in critical demand across medical devices, aerospace, defense, and manufacturing. These roles require a mindset — documentation discipline, traceability, risk awareness — that is rarely developed in academic settings and takes years to build on the job.

✚WEF: Quality control differentiates growing from declining roles. Engineering workforce trends show persistent shortages in defense, aerospace, and advanced manufacturing. AI governance creating new regulatory demand.
9

Venture Economics

Work Products

Engineering graduates consistently lack business acumen. The ability to understand cost drivers, make design decisions with financial implications in mind, and communicate in business terms bridges the gap between technical execution and organizational value creation that employers prize.

✚Business and financial operations are among the top-3 occupation groups for AI skill demand (McKinsey). Financial literacy and cost-aware design are consistently cited as gaps in engineering graduate readiness.
10

Patent & IP Intelligence

Work Products

In medical device commercialization, IP literacy is a differentiating skill. Employers in regulated industries need engineers who can conduct patent landscape analyses, understand freedom-to-operate assessments, and recognize when inventions are patentable — skills that accelerate the path from bench to market.

✚Patent filings in medical devices have grown 12% annually. Companies with strong IP portfolios command higher valuations. IP-literate engineers reduce legal costs and accelerate commercialization timelines.
11

Collaborative Professional Practice

Reflection & Coaching

Teamwork is the #2 employer-sought skill, but the gap is deep. Graduates report passive skills like listening and time management as top workplace skills, while employers prioritize active collaboration — initiative, co-creation, constructive conflict, and cross-cultural teamwork.

✚NACE 2025: Teamwork is the #2 sought skill. ASCE: Effective collaboration can increase project effectiveness by 30%. 65% of employers say engineering graduates lack adequate collaboration skills.
12

Professional Growth & Adaptability

Reflection & Coaching

With 39% of core skills expected to change by 2030, employers need workers who can learn continuously, transfer skills across domains, and operate productively under ambiguity. The traditional model of "train once, deploy forever" is obsolete — adaptability is the skill that keeps all other skills current.

✚WEF: Resilience, flexibility and agility is the #2 core skill. Curiosity and lifelong learning is #8. 59 of 100 workers need training by 2030. Skills change 66% faster in AI-exposed jobs (PwC).
13

Ethical Reasoning & Responsible Innovation

Reflection & Coaching

Employers in healthcare, technology, and regulated industries increasingly require professionals who can identify and navigate ethical tensions in product development. As AI and medical devices raise new questions about bias, access, privacy, and safety, ethical reasoning has moved from a nice-to-have to a core hiring criterion.

✚WEF 2025: Responsible technology governance is a fast-emerging employer need. 70% of healthcare companies now include ethics in job descriptions for engineering roles. Regulatory bodies increasingly require documented ethical review in device submissions.
